Let $\cal{B}_1,\dots,\cal{B}_L$ be the set of basic variables during the simplex method starting at Phase 2. Define a $p$-simplex $\sigma$ as **active** at step $i$ if the $x_\sigma^+-x_\sigma^-\ne0$. Likewise, a $(p+1)$-simplex $\tau$ is active if $y_\tau^+-y_\tau^-\ne0$. Define the following functions
$$
	f(\sigma) = \begin{cases}
		i & \text{$\sigma$ transistions from inactive to active at step $i$} \\
		L+1 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
$$
and
$$
	g(\sigma) = \begin{cases}
		i & \text{$\sigma$ transistions from active to inactive at step $i$} \\
		L+1 & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
$$

**Conjecture:** $f$ and $g$ are well-defined. That is, a simplex only becomes active once and only becomes inactive once.

**Conjecture:** For any $p$-simplex $\sigma$, it follows that $g(\sigma)=i<L+1$ if and only if there is a $(p+1)$-simplex $\tau>\sigma$ such that $f(\tau)=i$. We denote this pairing by $\{\sigma<\tau\}$ and the set of such pairings we denote by $V$. Consider the Hasse diagram for $K$ (excluding the $\emptyset$). For each pairing $\{\sigma<\tau\}\in V$, flip the direction of the edge in the diagram so that $\sigma\rightarrow\tau$ (see example below). Then $V$ forms an acyclic partial matching of the modified Hasse diagram.

![[hasse_morse_ex.png#invert | center]]

**Figure:** Modified Hasse diagram (see example given in [[2026-05-26]]) for partial matching $V=\{\{[0,1]<[0,1,4]\},\{[1,4]<[0,1,4]\},\{[0,3]<[0,3,4]\}\}$ (shown in red) showing the lack of a cycle.

We call the unpaired simplices **critical**. Denote by $u_p$ the number of critical $p$-simplices. Then it follows by discrete Morse theory ([Theorem 6.3](https://webhomes.maths.ed.ac.uk/~v1ranick/papers/forman1.pdf)) that our complex is homotopy equivalent to a CW-complex with exactly $u_p$ cells of dimension $p$, for each $p\ge0$. As a consequence: if $V$ is a complete acyclic matching, then our complex $K$ is contractible which matches our intuition.

---

**Update:** While $f$ and $g$ appear to be well-defined, the pairings $V$ do not always form a partial matching. This is even visible in the above picture... However, it does appear that the each simplex appears in a pair at most twice, which may be a result of codimension 2 as $p$-simplices touch at most two $(p+1)$-simplices.
