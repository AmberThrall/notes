By Cramer's rule,
$$
	Bd_B = -A_j \Rightarrow d_i = \frac{\det(B(i:-A_j))}{\det(B)}
$$
where $B(i:-A_j)$ is the matrix formed by replacing the $i$-th column of $B$ by $-A_j$. Thus, $d_i$ is integral if
$$
	\det(B)\mid \det(B(i:-A_j)).
$$
Based on the above log:
$$
	\det(B(i:-A_j))=-\det(B)=-1.
$$
whenever $x_i>0$.

Moreover, based on further logs we get the following conjecture:

**Conjecture:** Assume doing the current iteration, the minimum-ratio test gives $\theta^*>0$. Then $\det(B)=1$.

Note that $\det(B)$ is not always 1. In some iterations, $\det(B)>1$, but they had $\theta^*=0$. Thus, we could rewrite the conjecture as follows:

**Conjecture:** If $\det(B)\ne 1$, then there is a basic variable $i$ such that $x_i=0$.

Note that $\det(B)=1$ directly implies that $d$ is integral as
$$
	d_B = -B^{-1}A_j = -\frac{1}{\det(B)}\textup{adj}(B)A_j
$$
where $\textup{adj}(B)$ and $A_j$ are both integral. Further work would be necessary to show that $\theta^*\in\Z$ is integral when $\det(B)=1$. One might be able to argue that $\det(B)=1$ implies that $d\in\{-1,0,1\}^n$.

Again by Cramer's rule,
$$
	x_i = \frac{\det(B(i:b))}{\det(B)}.
$$
So the question is, what causes $B(i:b)$ to become singular? Naturally, if $B(i:b)$ is singular, then $b\in\textup{span}\{B_j:j\ne i\}$, i.e., there are coefficients $c_1,\dots,c_m\in\R$ such that
$$
	\sum_{j\ne i}c_jB_j = b.
$$
It is not clear how $\det(B)\ne 1$ relates to this. Some ideas:
- Zero would be an eigenvalue of $B(i:b)^\top$:
	- There is some vector $v\in\R^n$ such that $B_j^\top v=0$ for all $j\ne i$ and $B_i^\top v\ne 0$.
	- $b\perp\textup{span}\{B_j:j\ne i\}$
- Expansion along column $i$ for $B(i:b)$ and $B$
- Anything to say about the lattice $B\Z^n=\{Bx\mid x\in\Z^n\}$?
