I was investigating the notion of torsion in relation to the flat norm LP as suggested by [Bala's conversation with Claude](https://claude.ai/share/f8f11717-f51e-44fc-8d12-18d522499fba) and I believe I came up with a counterexample.

I took a triangulation of $\R P^2$, fattened it up by replacing each triangle of $\R P^2$ with a tetrahedra where the fourth point is given by the barycentric coordinates of the triangle. I then randomly generated input chains until I found one that gave fractional solutions. I set $\lambda=0.05$. I did not use the area of the triangles as weights, I simply set the weights to one.

![[SS_2026-05-05_1778014831.png#invert | center]]

For the triangles ($y$-values) in the output, the yellow triangles belong to the original $\R P^2$ and have a coefficient of $\pm 0.5$. The orange triangles are protruding out of the $\R P^2$ along the created tetrahedra and have a coefficient of $\pm 1$.

The above can be ignored, this is codimension 3.

However, I may have found something of interest. I took an instance which had several instances of $|\det(B)|\ne 1$, where $B$ is the basis matrix for an intermediate step. In each case, I computed the Smith normal form of $B$ and found they all had the following form:
$$
	SNF(B) = S = \begin{bmatrix}
		I_{m-t} \\
		& k I_t
	\end{bmatrix}
$$
where $k\in\Z_+$ and $0\le t<m$. The values of $k$ and $t$ vary across intermediate bfs's. 

Consider the lattice generated by $B$:
$$
	\Lambda(B) = \{Bz: z\in\Z^m\} \subset \Z^m.
$$
Consider then the cokernel, $\Z^m/\Lambda(B)$, which has index $|\det(B)|$. From the above, it appears that our cokernel is of the form $\Z^m/\Lambda(B)\cong(\Z/k\Z)^t$. I further claim (without real evidence) that this is a $p$-group. 

Recall that our bfs $Bx_B=b$ has integral solution $x_B$ if and only if $b\in\Lambda(B)$. Let $b'=Ub$ where $UBV=S$. Then $x_B = VS^{-1}b'$. Note that $V$ is unimodular, so $x_B\in\Z^m$ if and only if $S^{-1}b'\in\Z^{m}$. Thus, $x_B\in\Z^m$ if and only if
$$
	(Ub)_i \equiv 0~(\bmod k),~\forall i> m-t.
$$

Numerical experiments also suggest that 
$$
	G_Bb\equiv 0\bmod k
$$
where $G_B$ is the last $t$-rows of $U$ for every corresponding basis $B$. However, upon checking it appears that
$$
	G_B\not\equiv 0\bmod k,
$$
i.e., there exists some vector $b\in\Z^m$ such that $G_Bb\not\equiv 0\bmod k$. However, there is no guarantee that if we start with such a right-hand side $b$ that the corresponding basis $B$ for $G_B$ would appear. This suggests that the LP's polyhedra may not be integral for all $b\in\Z^m$.

Define
$$
	\cal{B}_B = \begin{cases}
	\Z^m & \text{if } |\det(B)|=1 \\
	\{b\in\Z^m: G_Bb\equiv0\bmod k_B\} & \text{otherwise}.
	\end{cases}
$$
Then we know the set of inputs $b\in\Z^m$ which give integral solutions contains the sublattice
$$
	\cal{B} = \bigcap_{\text{$m\times m$ minors $B$}}\cal{B}_B \subsetneq \Z^m,
$$
but is likely much larger as not all basis $B$ are visited. That is,
$$
	\cal{B}\subseteq \{b\in\Z^m:\text{LP gives integral solution}\}\subseteq\Z^m.
$$
